Based on the design documents and repo, here's an honest assessment of decision density per in-game day: [github](https://github.com/JacobStephens2/clowder-and-crest)

**What decisions exist each day:**

1. **Job selection** — Pick from the refreshed board, weighing category, difficulty, reward, and stat fit against your available cats
2. **Cat-job matching** — Which cat to assign, considering stat match %, trait/mood modifiers, and whether you want to build a combo chain (same cat + same category on consecutive days for up to +25% reward)
3. **Solve vs. Station** — Active puzzle (higher reward, risk of failure penalty) vs. passive income (but locks the cat and has diminishing returns after 5 days)
4. **Puzzle execution** — Skill-based performance across 4 minigame types determines star rating and reward multiplier
5. **Economy triage** — Feed cats (2 fish/day each) or risk mood drops; save for rooms/furniture/recruits; manage fish crisis risk (2 broke days = a cat leaves)
6. **Daily cat wish** — One random cat per day has a fulfillable wish for mood/bond/stat rewards
7. **Reputation shaping** — Job category choices shift Crest vs. Shadow reputation, affecting recruit costs and income bonuses
8. **Merchant decisions** — Every 3rd day, buy special items or save fish
9. **Station crisis response** — ~10% daily chance of an event requiring intervention (Chapter 3+)
10. **When to end the day** — Push for one more job or bank your progress

**Where it risks feeling mechanical:**

- **Early game (Ch. 1-2) is thin.** With 1-2 cats and only pest control/courier jobs, you're mostly just picking the one available cat for the one best job. The combo chain and reputation systems don't have enough inputs yet to create tension. The first several days likely feel like going through the motions.
- **No auto-resolve means every job requires a minigame.** This is a double-edged sword — it keeps you engaged, but after 15+ puzzles the minigames themselves can become the "motions" you're going through, especially Rush Hour and Sokoban which are structurally similar each time.
- **One job per cat per day is a hard cap.** With 5 cats max, your decision ceiling is ~5 assignments per 3-minute day. That's not many.
- **Food cost is flat and predictable** (2 fish/cat/day), so the economy rarely forces hard tradeoffs once you're earning medium-job rewards.

**Where it works:**

- **Mid-to-late game stacks decisions well.** With 5 cats, combo chains to maintain, reputation to manage, station crises, merchant timing, bond thresholds approaching, and the Rat Plague pressure — a single day can have 6-8 genuinely distinct choices.
- **The fish crisis mechanic** adds real stakes to what could otherwise be rote optimization.
- **Job combo chains** reward planning across days, not just within them — this is the strongest "meaningful decision" lever because it creates tension between optimal stat matching today vs. maintaining a streak.

**Recommendations to increase decision density:**

1. **Early game needs a second axis of tension.** Consider giving the player a meaningful either/or choice in Ch. 1 — e.g., two jobs available but only time/energy for one, where both have narrative consequences.
2. **Vary the minigame selection** — let the player choose which minigame type to attempt (at different risk/reward), rather than random assignment. That's a free decision point.
3. **Add a "rest" action for cats** that actively recovers mood faster than passive recovery, creating a work-vs-rest tradeoff each day.
4. **Make food cost scale or vary** — seasonal scarcity, merchant markup, spoilage — so the upkeep decision isn't always trivial.

**Bottom line:** Days 1-10 likely feel like going through the motions. From Chapter 3 onward, there are enough intersecting systems (combos, reputation, crises, bonds, economy) to make most days feel like they matter. The design is solid in the mid-game — the gap is in the onramp.
