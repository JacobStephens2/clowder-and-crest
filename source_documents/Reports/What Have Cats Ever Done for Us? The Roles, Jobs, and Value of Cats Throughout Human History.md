# What Have Cats Ever Done for Us? The Roles, Jobs, and Value of Cats Throughout Human History

What jobs / tasks / value to humanity / role in human life have cats played over time? What's the closest thing to work they've had? From their own perspective and from a human perspective?

## Overview

Cats are unique in the animal kingdom: they are the only domesticated species that largely domesticated *themselves*. Over roughly 10,000 years, they have served humanity as pest controllers, sacred deities, military assets, emotional healers, cultural symbols, and internet celebrities — all while pursuing their own independent agenda. Understanding their contributions requires holding two perspectives simultaneously: what humans got out of the deal, and what cats were actually doing from their own point of view.

***

## Origins: A Self-Interested Merger

The story begins in the Fertile Crescent, roughly 8,000–10,000 years ago, when humans first began storing grain in large quantities. Those grain stores attracted rodents, and rodents attracted wildcats (*Felis lybica*) out of their natural habitats. Crucially, neither party planned this relationship — it emerged from mutual convenience. Cats got a reliable, concentrated food source and safety from larger predators; humans got free pest control.[1][2][3]

Because friendlier cats got table scraps and human protection on top of the hunting opportunities, tameness became evolutionarily advantageous. This is why scientists often say cats "domesticated themselves" — they voluntarily adopted behaviors that allowed them to thrive near humans, without humans systematically breeding them for docility the way dogs were bred. One of the most compelling archaeological markers of this bond is a 9,500-year-old grave discovered in Cyprus in 2004, where a cat was buried beside its human owner — suggesting emotional attachment, not just utility.[3][4][1]

## From the Cat's Perspective: What Has This Always Been About?

Before cataloguing what cats have *done for us*, it's worth asking what they were doing from their own perspective — because the answer fundamentally reframes everything else.

Cats are obligate carnivores and solitary hunters. Their motivation to hunt is not primarily driven by hunger — it is a hard-wired behavioral sequence (stalk, pounce, capture, kill) that runs on its own track, independent of satiation. Wild cats can make 10–20 kills per day to meet energy needs. The hunting instinct does not diminish after neutering because it is not hormonal — it is neurological.[5][6]

From the cat's perspective, human civilization was not a job offer. It was a hunting ground with bonus features: predictable prey density, shelter, and eventually supplemental food. The cat's "work" — pest control — was never labor in any meaningful sense for them. It was play, practice, and biological necessity colliding with human need. The fact that humans found this useful was, from the cat's vantage point, beside the point.

What cats have seemingly "chosen" over millennia is a life of **managed wildness** — retaining autonomous behavior, solitary instincts, and territorial nature, while selectively leveraging human proximity for benefit. Unlike dogs, which co-evolved to read and respond to human social cues as part of their survival strategy within a pack structure, cats remain largely socially independent. Research suggests they view their human caregivers somewhat like an ever-present parent figure — a source of food, warmth, and security — rather than as a partner or pack member.[7][4]

## The Core Job: Pest Control Across Civilizations

### Ancient Egypt

In ancient Egypt, the cat's pest-control role elevated it to near-divine status. Cats were sacred to the goddess Bastet, protector of the home, fertility, and domestic harmony. At the height of Bastet's popularity, killing a cat — even accidentally — was punishable by death. Cats wore gold jewelry in wealthy households and were fed from their owners' tables. When they died, families shaved their eyebrows in mourning.[8][9][10]

However, new archaeological research has complicated the grain-storage origin story. A 2025 study challenges the idea that domestication began in farming villages and argues it originated in Egypt in the second to first millennium BC — driven less by rodent control and more by *religious sacrifice*. Millions of cats were bred specifically for mummification and ritual sacrifice at temples dedicated to Bastet; so many were exported to Victorian Britain that tonnes of cat mummies were ground up as fertilizer. This suggests cats occupied two simultaneous roles in Egypt: divine symbols and working animals — with the "divine" role perhaps being primary.[11]

### Medieval Europe: From Monks' Companions to the Devil's Familiars

In medieval Europe, cats were the grain-store workers of choice, so valuable that an adult cat fetched the same price as a full-grown sheep, goat, or untrained dog. A kitten was worth as much as a piglet or lamb. They were the only animals permitted in monasteries, where religious manuscripts regularly depicted cats hunting mice. Cathedral records — such as those at Exeter Cathedral — show quarterly payments for "the cat," with occasional doubles suggesting two cats were on payroll simultaneously.[12][13]

Then came the Witch Craze. From the 16th century onward, cats — especially black cats — became associated with witchcraft, heresy, and the devil himself. Pope Innocent VIII declared in 1484 that the cat was "the devil's favourite animal and idol of all witches". This association led to widespread persecution of cats across Europe, a grim irony that may have indirectly worsened the Black Death: fewer cats meant more rats, and more rats meant more plague vectors. The pest-control value of cats was most visible precisely when it was absent.[14][13]

### Ships and the Age of Discovery

Cats went to sea almost as soon as humans did. Ship's cats date back approximately 9,000 years, initially used by ancient Egyptians to hunt birds on riverbanks. As ocean travel expanded, cats became standard crew on sailing ships — controlling rodents that would otherwise eat through food stores, rope, and cargo. It is believed that domestic cats spread throughout much of the world *because of* sailing ships during the Age of Discovery in the 15th–18th centuries. Some sailors viewed polydactyl cats (those with extra toes) as particularly desirable for their supposed superior balance on deck.[15][2]

## Military Service: Trenches, Gas Detection, and Messenger Cats

In both World Wars, cats served on the front lines in multiple capacities.[16][17]

During WWI, an estimated **500,000 cats** were deployed in the trenches of the Western Front. Their primary role was rodent control — protecting soldiers' food supplies from rats that infested the front. But they served two other remarkable functions:[17][18]

- **Gas detection**: Britain sent cats to the front as early warning systems for chemical weapons — particularly mustard gas. Because cats succumb to toxic gas faster than humans, their distress gave soldiers enough warning to don their gas masks. This was the same logic as "canaries in a coal mine."[18][17]
- **Morale and companionship**: Soldiers adopted cats as emotional anchors in horrifying conditions. Troops shared rations with cats, played with them between conflicts, and used them as psychological relief.[19][17]
- **Messengers**: During the Christmas Truce of 1914, soldiers on both sides tied messages around cats' collars and sent them across no man's land — exploiting the fact that cats "crossed no man's land freely".[17][18]

The cat Felix was famously sentenced to death for treason after British intelligence feared the free-roaming cats might be enemy spies — a testament to how seriously their movement across lines was taken.[18]

## Formal Employment: Paid Government Mousers

Cats have held real, if unofficial, salaried positions in government institutions for centuries. The British government has maintained official "Chief Mousers" at major institutions since at least the 1800s. Today, Larry the cat holds the title of Chief Mouser to the Cabinet Office at 10 Downing Street, while Palmerston serves the Foreign Office. Their "salary" historically consisted of food and the innards of captured rodents, though National Archives records show some cats received formal allowances.[20]

This practice was not merely ceremonial. Rodent infestations in government buildings, libraries, warehouses, and churches posed genuine threats to document preservation, food stores, and structural integrity. Cats were the most effective and cost-efficient solution available for centuries.

Modern working cats still operate across diverse environments — breweries, barns, warehouses, and offices. Humane societies in cities like Boston have formal "working cat programs" that place feral cats unsuitable for domestic adoption into commercial spaces as pest controllers. It is, in a very real sense, the oldest continuing employment relationship between humans and any animal.[21][22]

## Healing and Health: The Science of the Purr

One of the most surprising and scientifically validated contributions cats make is biological healing — though, again, this was never their *intention*.

A cat's purr operates at a frequency of 25 to 150 Hz. Research has shown that vibrations in this frequency range:[23][24]
- Promote healing of bones and tissue regeneration[25][23]
- Reduce inflammation and improve joint mobility[25]
- Aid tendon and muscle repair[23]

This may explain why cats heal remarkably quickly from their own injuries and why joint problems and bone cancer are rare in cats. More directly relevant to humans, vibration therapy using these same frequencies has been used to enhance bone repair and strengthen tendons in clinical settings. Spending time with a purring cat may thus provide low-level therapeutic benefit — particularly for those recovering from injuries or managing chronic pain.[24][25]

Beyond the purr, cat ownership is associated with a **30% reduction in the risk of heart disease**. Studies consistently show cats reduce anxiety, lower cortisol, and alleviate loneliness — particularly in older adults and children with autism, ADHD, and other cognitive conditions.[26][27]

## Therapy and Emotional Support: A Recognized Profession

Formal therapy-cat programs now operate across the United States, with over 200 registered therapy cats providing services in hospitals, counseling offices, long-term care facilities, and schools. A 2025 Washington State University study published in *Animals* found that therapy cats share specific behavioral traits with therapy dogs: high sociability, attention-seeking behavior, and tolerance for handling — including being picked up, which most cats resist. These cats are not just placid animals — they are a behaviorally distinct subset suited to the work.[28][29]

Emotional support cats serve a related but distinct function: providing individualized, continuous support to a single owner navigating mental health conditions, PTSD, depression, and anxiety. Unlike therapy cats, emotional support cats accompany their owners into the world — traveling, navigating public spaces, and adapting to novel environments.[28]

## Cultural and Religious Roles: Symbol and Deity

Cats have occupied a symbolic and spiritual role in human culture that no other domesticated animal matches in diversity:

| Culture / Era | Role | Nature of Role |
|---|---|---|
| Ancient Egypt | Sacred to Bastet, divine protector | Religious, spiritual, sacrificial |
| Medieval Europe | Devil's familiar, witch's companion | Feared, persecuted |
| Monasteries (medieval) | Companion and pest controller | Practical, warmly regarded |
| Japan | *Maneki-neko* (beckoning cat), symbol of fortune | Talisman, commercial symbol |
| WWI/WWII | Mascot, morale booster, gas detector | Military utility and emotional comfort |
| Modern Western culture | Beloved pet, emotional anchor | Companion, family member |
| Internet era | Meme, celebrity, community anchor | Cultural phenomenon, entertainment |

In Japan, the cat's image as a symbol of luck and cuteness (*kawaii*) is so dominant that it has shaped internet culture globally — because Japan and the United States together drove the early internet. The cat's ascent online began in digital chatrooms of the 1990s and exploded with LOLcats and the *I Can Has Cheezburger* phenomenon of 2007.[30]

***

## The Internet Era: Unpaid Influencers, Massive Cultural Value

The modern cat's "job" — if it can be called that — is cultural and emotional labor, mediated by social media. Cats like Nala Cat (a shelter rescue) have built followings in the millions, launched product lines, and raised significant funds for animal welfare causes. Cat content accounts for an enormous share of internet traffic, driving engagement across platforms, generating advertising revenue, and building communities of people who might otherwise feel socially isolated.[31][32][30]

Psychologically, cat memes function as contemporary icons — vehicles for self-expression and anthropomorphized emotional projection. The cats themselves are oblivious to this. Their contribution is entirely involuntary. Yet the cultural value — the community, the emotional relief, the shared identity of "cat person" — is real and substantial.[32][30]

## The Honest Accounting: Two Balanced Ledgers

### From a Human Perspective
Cats have provided:
- **Pest control** protecting food stores, preventing structural damage, and limiting disease spread for ~10,000 years
- **Military utility** as gas detectors, morale boosters, and informal messengers in wartime
- **Religious and cultural symbolism** across virtually every major civilization
- **Measurable health benefits** including cardiovascular protection, stress reduction, and vibrational healing
- **Therapeutic services** formalized into recognized care roles
- **Emotional companionship** — alleviating loneliness, supporting children with developmental conditions, and providing psychological stability
- **Cultural and economic value** through art, internet content, brand partnerships, and commercial mascotry

### From a Cat's Perspective
The cat's ledger looks different:
- Cats were *never working*. They were hunting — a hardwired biological imperative that happened to align with human needs.
- Proximity to humans was a strategic choice: reliable food, shelter, and protection from larger predators, with minimal obligation to cooperate.
- The "domestication" is arguably incomplete — cats retained their solitary hunting behavior, territorial instincts, and preference for autonomy in ways no domesticated dog would.[4]
- What cats receive from humans today — food, shelter, veterinary care, warmth, and play — maps closely to what a mother provides a kitten. The human became a permanent caregiver in the cat's social world.[7]
- The relationship, from the cat's side, resembles a **long-running, mutually beneficial arrangement that they opted into and retain the right to exit** — which is, perhaps, the most cat thing imaginable.

***

## Conclusion

The cat's contribution to humanity spans grain silos and battlefields, temples and therapy offices, medieval monasteries and viral meme accounts. What makes it remarkable is the underlying consistency: in nearly every era and culture, cats have provided exactly what they were always going to do anyway — hunt, exist with sovereign independence, and occasionally purr — and humans found ways to layer enormous meaning, utility, and comfort onto that behavior. The cat did not change to serve us. We changed to accommodate them. And that, more than any job title or task, may be the most significant thing cats have ever done.
